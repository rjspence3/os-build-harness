1. Executive Summary
Rivian is modernizing a set of enterprise applications that currently run on Appian and on Microsoft Power Platform. This document specifies the first of three flagship applications in that initiative: a Supplier & Parts Onboarding Platform that governs how new suppliers and their parts are qualified, approved, and released to Rivian's manufacturing lines.
The current onboarding process is complex, cross-functional, and slow. It spans procurement, quality, engineering, compliance, and manufacturing readiness, and it depends on integrations to PLM and ERP as well as a mix of manual, email- and spreadsheet-driven steps. Rivian's objectives are to (1) retire the incumbent Appian workflow, (2) materially reduce Microsoft/Power Platform dependency, and (3) deliver a governed, auditable, and faster onboarding experience that fits Rivian's evolving AI development lifecycle (AIDLC) and reference architecture.
The proposed solution rebuilds the process on OutSystems Developer Cloud (ODC) as a governed application with native workflow orchestration, embedded compliance and security controls, and first-class integrations to Rivian's PLM and ERP systems. The platform is designed so that governance, deployment, and observability are built in rather than assembled by hand — reducing the security-review and release friction Rivian has flagged, and positioning the app as a reference pattern for the other two applications in the initiative.
This is a hybrid document: Sections 2–5 frame the business problem, scope, and target solution; Sections 6–10 specify functional and non-functional requirements, integrations, and data; and Sections 11–15 cover migration, governance/AIDLC fit, phasing, and open questions.

3. Current State & Problem Statement
3.1 How onboarding works today
Supplier and parts onboarding is a multi-stage, multi-team process that currently spans an Appian workflow, Microsoft Power Platform / SharePoint data capture, PLM (part and change data), ERP (vendor master), and a significant amount of manual coordination over email and spreadsheets. Requirements for the process are only partially documented and have in places been reverse-engineered from the legacy tooling.
3.2 Key pain points
	•	Fragmented tooling: intake, qualification, and approvals are split across Appian, Power Platform, and manual channels, with no single source of truth for supplier/part status.
	•	Slow cycle time: sequential hand-offs between procurement, quality, engineering, and compliance extend onboarding and delay part availability to the lines.
	•	Governance friction: security and compliance reviews are lengthy (reviews have run well beyond target, in some cases 20+ days), and audit evidence must be reconstructed manually.
	•	Skill and continuity risk: platform knowledge is concentrated in contractors rather than full-time employees, creating key-person and knowledge-transfer risk.
	•	Integration gaps: PLM and ERP synchronization is incomplete, and business stakeholders report missing integrations and unhandled exception paths.
	•	Feedback loops are manual: stakeholder feedback (e.g., from quality or legal) is captured off-platform and re-entered by hand, rather than routed automatically back into the workflow.
4. Solution Overview
4.1 Target solution
A single, governed Supplier & Parts Onboarding application built on OutSystems Developer Cloud (ODC). The application provides a supplier-facing intake experience, an internal case-management and workflow engine for qualification and approvals, and governed, auditable integrations to Rivian's PLM and ERP systems and identity provider. Governance, deployment, observability, and security controls are provided natively by the platform.

5. Scope
5.1 In scope
	•	Supplier intake and self-service portal for new supplier and part submissions.
	•	Tiered supplier qualification workflow (critical vs. standard) with parallel functional reviews.
	•	Automated compliance screening (denied-party/sanctions) with review-on-match handling.
	•	Part introduction / release-to-line readiness tied to PLM part and change data.
	•	Approval, activation, and creation/update of the approved supplier in the ERP vendor master.
	•	Requalification and periodic review triggers.
	•	In-app feedback capture routed automatically into the workflow (replacing off-platform forms).
	•	Role-based access, full audit trail, dashboards, and reporting.
	•	Migration of the process off Appian and off Microsoft Power Platform for this workflow.
5.2 Out of scope (this phase)
	•	Replacement of the PLM or ERP systems themselves (integrated, not replaced).
	•	Supplier performance management / scorecarding beyond qualification (candidate for a later phase).
	•	The other two flagship applications in the initiative (this app sets the pattern for them).
	•	Migration of historical Appian case data beyond an agreed cut-over set (see Section 11).

10. Migration Approach (Appian & Power Platform)
OutSystems uses its own model and runtime; there is no automated import of an Appian or Power Platform application. Migration is therefore a governed rebuild on ODC, not a lift-and-shift, using the incumbent systems as reference for requirements and business rules. This is an opportunity to close the documented requirement gaps rather than replicate them.
10.1 Approach
	•	Discovery: confirm the as-is process, rules, and integrations from Appian/Power Platform and stakeholder interviews; finalize the requirement-gap list.
	•	Rebuild on ODC: implement the target workflow, data model, and integrations; accelerate initial build using platform AI generation from the finalized requirements, with human review.
	•	Data migration: migrate open/in-flight cases and reference data per an agreed cut-over set; historical closed cases archived or read-only as agreed.
	•	Parallel/cut-over: run a controlled cut-over with rollback; decommission the Appian workflow and the Power Platform intake for this process.
10.2 What is explicitly not carried over
	•	Undocumented workarounds and manual spreadsheet steps — replaced by governed workflow.
	•	Off-platform feedback capture — replaced by in-app feedback routing.
11. Governance & AIDLC Fit
The solution is designed to slot into Rivian's reference architecture and AI development lifecycle as the governed platform layer for this class of application — not to replace Rivian's AIDLC. Rivian's teams and preferred front-end AI tooling remain in place; the platform provides the built-in machinery that makes AI-accelerated app delivery safe and releasable.
